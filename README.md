Employee Attrition Prediction using Machine Learning 
To better understand our workforce dynamics and proactively address employee turnover, we conducted a comprehensive analysis of historical HR data. The objective was to identify the underlying patterns of why employees leave and to build a predictive tool that flags "flight-risk" personnel before they hand in their resignation.
Key Drivers of Attrition: Why Are They Leaving?
Our analysis revealed that salary alone does not dictate an employee's decision to leave. Instead, turnover is driven by a combination of compensation, burnout, and career stage. The top three predictors of attrition are:
Monthly Income: Unsurprisingly, base compensation is the strongest anchor for retention. Lower-paid employees are leaving at significantly higher rates.
Chronic Overtime: Employees who consistently work overtime are at a severe risk of burnout and subsequent exit, regardless of their pay bracket.
Age & Tenure: Younger employees, particularly those in their first two years with the company, represent the highest flight risk. Retention stabilizes significantly as employees mature within the organization.
High-Risk Areas: Where Should We Focus?
The data clearly points to specific areas of the business that require immediate HR intervention:
The Sales Department: This division is experiencing the highest overall attrition rate (over 20%).
Job Roles: Specifically, Sales Representatives (nearly 40% exit rate) and Laboratory Technicians (roughly 24% exit rate) are turning over at alarming speeds compared to the rest of the company.
Strategic HR Recommendations
Based on these findings, we recommend implementing the following targeted retention strategies:
Mandatory Overtime Audits: Implement a strict policy requiring secondary managerial approval for sustained overtime, specifically targeting Sales Representatives and Laboratory Technicians. We must structurally prevent burnout before it results in turnover.
Targeted "Stay Interviews": Launch a proactive outreach program for younger employees approaching their 1-to-2-year anniversary. Engaging these employees during their highest flight-risk window allows HR to address concerns regarding compensation, environment, and career progression before they start interviewing elsewhere.
System Limitations
While this predictive analysis provides a powerful early-warning system for HR, it is important to note its limitations. The system relies entirely on historical, quantifiable data. It cannot predict qualitative, deeply human factors—such as a sudden personal family emergency, an unrecorded conflict with a new manager, or an unexpected career shift. Therefore, this tool should be used to guide proactive HR conversations, not to make definitive assumptions about an employee's loyalty.
